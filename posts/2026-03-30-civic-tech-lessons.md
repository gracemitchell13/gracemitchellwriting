After two years building Housing Navigator Massachusetts — a statewide housing search platform that now serves over 450,000 users annually — I've been doing a lot of thinking about what separates civic tech tools that actually get used from the ones that gather dust.

This isn't a retrospective. It's more a set of working principles I've arrived at, slowly, through iteration and failure.

## The tool has to solve the right problem

This sounds obvious until you're six months into building something and realize you've been solving for the agency's workflow, not the end user's. In housing tech, that's the difference between a tool that helps a case manager log referrals and a tool that helps a family find a home they can actually afford.

Both matter. Neither is a substitute for the other.

## Trust is infrastructure

In government digital services, trust isn't a soft outcome — it's load-bearing. Users who don't trust a platform won't enter accurate information, won't return, and won't recommend it. And in civic contexts, the stakes of a broken trust loop are higher than in commercial tech. A confused renter doesn't just churn; they may give up on the process entirely.

Building trust means **consistency over time**, clear language, and being honest about what a tool can and can't do.

## "Minimum viable" has limits in the public sector

The lean startup model of shipping early and iterating fast runs into a structural wall in government tech. Procurement cycles are long. Change management is hard. Users are often navigating crises and can't absorb frequent disruption.

This doesn't mean you don't iterate — it means your initial scope has to be much tighter, and your rollout plan has to be much more deliberate.
